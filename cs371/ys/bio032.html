How the Flu Virus Disarms Our Defenses

Friday August 24, 2012

Influenza Virus Particles
Image: CDC/Frederick Murphy

When viruses infect cells, they use the host's own genetic machinery to disable an immune response. Researchers from Northwestern University have discovered how the influenza virus disarms our natural immune response. While studying human lung cells infected with the influenza A virus, it was revealed that the virus decreases the production level of immune system proteins needed to initiate an immune response. The virus accomplishes this by activating microRNAs which function to shut down protein production.

According to the study's lead author Curt M. Horvath, "Our goal is to understand how the flu replicates in the host. Now we've discovered a new pathway in which the flu controls the immune response, by shutting down vital protein production. With better understanding of this mechanism, one day we may be able to customize therapeutics to target individual flu strains." The researchers identified two microRNAs that activate the specific genes IRAK1 and MAPK3. When activated, these genes decrease the levels of immune response initiating proteins in the body.

Blood Type Linked to Heart Disease

Thursday August 23, 2012

Researchers have found that your blood type could put you at a higher risk of developing heart disease. According to a study published in the journal Arteriosclerosis, Thrombosis and Vascular Biology, those with blood type AB have the highest risk for developing heart disease. Those with blood type B have the next highest risk, followed by type A. Those with blood type O have the lowest risk. The reasons for the link between blood type and heart disease are not fully understood; however, type AB blood has been linked to inflammation and type A to increased levels of a certain type of cholesterol. These two conditions can damage blood vessels.

According to the lead author of the study, Lu Qi, "While people cannot change their blood type, our findings may help physicians better understand who is at risk for developing heart disease." The researchers state that knowing your blood type can help you determine if you are at a higher risk for developing heart disease. Establishing healthy lifestyle habits can reduce the risk of developing heart disease.

Antibacterial Agent May Inhibit Muscle Activity

Friday August 17, 2012

Credit: Bill Branson / National Cancer Institute

Researchers have discovered that a substance found in items such as hand soaps, mouthwashes, deodorants, toys, and other household items inhibits muscle cell contractions. Triclosan, an antibacterial agent, was shown in animal lab tests to impair heart and skeletal muscle contractions. Triclosan has been detected in waterways, aquatic animals, human blood, urine, and breast milk.

According to researcher Nipavan Chiamvimonvat, "The effects of triclosan on cardiac function were really dramatic. Although triclosan is not regulated as a drug, this compound acts like a potent cardiac depressant in our models." The researchers previously discovered that triclosan can also interfere with reproductive hormone activity and signaling processes in the brain. They also caution that since these studies were conducted with animals, additional studies are necessary to determine if triclosan produces similar problems in humans.

Digestive System: Nutrient Absorption

Thursday August 16, 2012

Digestive System
Image: Don Bliss/National Cancer Institute

Have you ever wondered where all of those digested food molecules are absorbed in your body? Well, before those carbohydrates, proteins, and fats can be absorbed, they must be broken down into smaller molecules. Carbohydrate digestion begins in the mouth, protein digestion begins in the stomach, and fat digestion begins in the intestines.

Once these materials have been broken down, nutrient absorption can occur. As food is processed along the digestive tract, most of the nutrients are absorbed through the walls of the small intestines. The nutrients are carried into the bloodstream where they can travel to all parts of the body. Other substances that are absorbed during digestion include vitamins, water, and salt.
