The Digital Morphology library is a dynamic archive of information on digital morphology and high-resolution X-ray computed tomography of biological specimens. Browse through the site and see spectacular imagery and animations and details on the morphology of many representatives of the Earth's biota. Recent additions or updates to the site include:
Evolution of the Penguin Brain
Penguins (Sphenisciformes) underwent significant postcranial morphological changes during their evolutionary transition from aerial flight to underwater flight, but little is known of how this transition affected their neurosensory systems. In the September issue of Zoological Journal of the Linnean Society, Daniel Ksepka and coauthors generated and compared virtual endocasts of the early Miocene stem penguin Paraptenodytes antarcticus (shown here) and three extant penguin species, as well as two outgroup species (Gavia immer and Diomedea immutabilis, both also on DigiMorph).  [more...] 
Weird Bird Wings13 Jun 2012
Bucking the near-universal rule that long bones of the limbs are hollow, male club-winged manakins (Machaeropterus deliciosa) have solidified ulnae and humeri (in red) as revealed by HRXCT. These birds also have a pair of enlarged, dense feathers attaching directly to the ulna, which resonate to produce a distinctive sound used in courtship displays. As reported by Kim Bostwick and coauthors in a paper in press in Biology Letters, this represents a unique case of sexual selection for sound production at the cost of flight efficiency.  [more...] 
It. Is. Finished.30 May 2012
Seven years and ~215 HRXCT scans later, the final paper for the NSF Assembling the Tree of Life project for squamates, 'Deep Scaly,' is out! Published this spring by the Yale Peabody Museum Bulletin (and downloadable here), Gauthier and coauthors analyzed 610 phenotypic characters in 51 extinct and 141 extant species of squamates (lizards including snakes, and amphisbaenians) to ascertain the arrangement of the deeper branches of the tree. More than half of these taxa have been added to DigiMorph thus far; click 'more' for a list.  [more...] 
Uropeltid Snake Skulls02 Apr 2012
PLoS ONE recently published a paper by Olori and Bell on the cranial morphology of uropeltids, a highly-derived clade of fossorial snakes that may shed light on the early evolution of that lineage. The authors emphasized the study of disarticulated elements and multiple individuals, including this scan of Uropeltis woodmasoni, Woodmason's earth snake. Learn more about their study by reading this new DigiMorph account.  [more...] 
Suckermouth Armored Catfishes24 Jan 2012
Loricariidae is a clade of detritivorous-herbivorous neotropical freshwater fishes that are unique in having a ventral oral disk and jaws that are specialized for benthic feeding and surface attachment. In the January 2012 issue of Journal of Morphology, Nathan Lujan and Jonathan Armbruster examine the anatomy and functional morphology of this apparatus in 25 loricariid species, three of which can be found on DigiMorph: Panaque nigrolineatus (shown here), Chaetostoma cf. milesi, and Leporacanthicus joselimai.  [more...] 
Alioramus altai, Tyrannosauroid18 Nov 2011
Alioramus altai is a new species of tyrannosauroid that exhibits features intermediate between the basal theropod and avialan conditions that optimize as the ancestral condition for Coelurosauria�a diverse group of derived theropods that includes modern birds. The image at right shows the braincase of Alioramus rendered semi-transparent, with sinuses and the cranial cavity rendered as multicolored endocasts. Bever and coauthors use this CT imagery to analyze and describe this new taxon in a recently published study.  [more...]
